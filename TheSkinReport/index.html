5 Reasons Your Heel Cream Isn't Working (And the One Ingredient That Will)

We spent 3 months testing every heel repair product on the market. Here's what the $800-a-year foot cream industry doesn't want you to know.

By Kate Sullivan · 7 min read · Updated March 2026

If you've ever bought a heel cream, used it religiously for weeks, and still had cracked heels — you're not alone. And it's not your fault.

The problem isn't that you're not trying hard enough. It's that almost every product on the shelf is fundamentally incapable of fixing cracked heels. Here's why — and what actually works.

1

The concentration is too low

This is the biggest one. Most heel creams contain urea — a dermatologist-recommended ingredient that breaks down hard, dead skin. But here's what the label doesn't tell you:

Urea only works as a skin repair agent above 40% concentration. Below that, it's just a moisturiser. It makes your skin feel softer for a few hours, but it doesn't actually dissolve the thick, cracked keratin underneath.

2

You're treating the symptom, not the cause

Cracked heels aren't a dryness problem. They're a keratin buildup problem.

Your body produces keratin — the same tough protein in your fingernails — and layers it onto your heels for protection. Over time, this layer gets thick, rigid, and dry. When it can't flex anymore, it cracks. Sometimes deep enough to bleed.

Most creams moisturise the surface of that thick layer. That's like painting over a cracked wall — it looks better for a day, but the structural damage is still there underneath.

What you actually need is something that breaks down the excess keratin itself. That's what clinical-strength urea does at 60%. It dissolves the cause, not just the symptom.

The Science

Why dermatologists recommend urea above 40%

Below 40%, urea acts as a humectant — it draws water into the skin. Helpful for general dryness, but it can't penetrate the thick keratin layer on heels.

Above 40%, urea becomes a keratolytic — it chemically breaks the bonds between dead skin cells, dissolving the hardened buildup that causes cracks, fissures, and roughness.

Clinical studies consistently point to 40–60% urea as the therapeutic range for hyperkeratotic skin conditions like cracked heels.

3

Filing and pedicures make it worse

This one hurts. Literally and figuratively.

When you aggressively file your heels — whether at a salon or with an electric foot file at home — you're removing the top layer of dead skin. Your heels look great for about 5 days.

But here's what happens next: your body interprets that filing as damage. So it overcompensates by building the keratin layer back faster and thicker than before. It's a vicious cycle. File → regrow thicker → file harder → regrow even thicker.

This is why people who get regular pedicures often feel like their heels are getting worse over time, not better. They are.

The solution isn't mechanical removal. It's chemical dissolution. When urea at 60% dissolves the keratin, your body doesn't trigger that overcompensation response. The thick layer simply breaks down and sheds naturally.
